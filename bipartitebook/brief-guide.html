A Brief Guide to the Bipartite Universe

The universe is split in two. There is the macroscopic world of gravity and thermodynamics, and the microscopic world of quantum mechanics. For a century, we tried to unify them. We failed. The Bipartite Universe Framework explains why.

1. The Architecture of Limits

Unlike string theory or loop quantum gravity, the Bipartite Framework doesn't invent new, unobservable dimensions. Instead, it looks at what the universe strictly forbids. We call these the Impossibility Theorems.

There are 16 absolute boundaries in physics. They prevent perpetual motion, time travel, faster-than-light communication, and free energy. They are not engineering problems to be solved; they are structural load-bearing walls of reality.

3. Why Do The Loopholes Fail?

Every single one of the 131 theoretical attacks failed. But why did they fail?

When we traced the failures back to their mathematical roots, we discovered they all crash into one of three absolute mathematical identities. The universe is protected by Stokes' Theorem, Lorentz Invariance, and the No-Cloning Theorem.

The 3 Closing Identities

All 131 loophole attempts eventually reduce to violating one of these three fundamental mathematical laws. They are not engineering limitations; they are structural boundaries of reality.

Identity 1: Stokes' Theorem

∮ F_conservative · dr = 0

You cannot extract free work from a conservative vacuum field. The vacuum is the ground state. Any loop returns to zero.

Identity 2: Lorentz Invariance (c-bound)

v_classical_channel ≤ c

Information and matter cannot propagate faster than the speed of light. Causality must be preserved.

Forces Structural Block On:T5 (FTL), T16 (Teleportation)

Identity 3: No-Cloning Theorem

ψ → ψ ⊗ ψ forbidden

Wootters and Zurek (1982) proved it is impossible to create an identical copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state.
